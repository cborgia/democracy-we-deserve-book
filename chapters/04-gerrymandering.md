# Chapter 4: Gerrymandering — Choosing Your Voters

In most democracies, voters choose their representatives. In America, representatives choose their voters.

This is gerrymandering — the practice of drawing electoral district boundaries to predetermine election outcomes — and it is the single most brazen manipulation in American politics. It's not hidden. It's not subtle. State legislators sit in a room with sophisticated mapping software, population data, and voter history files, and they draw lines on a map that guarantee their party wins the maximum number of seats regardless of how the public actually votes.

And it's perfectly legal.

## How It Works

Every ten years, after the census, congressional and state legislative district lines are redrawn to reflect population changes. In most states, this process is controlled by the state legislature — which means the party in power gets to draw the maps.

They use two primary techniques:

**Packing:** Concentrate opposition voters into as few districts as possible. Give them a handful of districts they win overwhelmingly (70-80%), while your party wins a larger number of districts by comfortable but not wasteful margins (55-60%).

**Cracking:** Split opposition voters across multiple districts so they don't form a majority in any of them. A city that would naturally elect two representatives gets divided into four districts, each anchored by enough rural voters to outvote the urban ones.

::: callout
**The math of manipulation:** Imagine a state with 1 million voters — 600,000 favor Party A and 400,000 favor Party B. In a fair map with 10 districts, you'd expect roughly 6 seats for Party A and 4 for Party B.

But with gerrymandering, Party B (despite having only 40% of voters) can draw a map that gives them 6 or 7 seats. Pack all 600,000 Party A voters into 3 districts (200,000 each, winning those districts with 100% of the vote). Spread Party B's 400,000 voters across the remaining 7 districts (roughly 57,000 each), filling the rest with the leftover Party A voters. Party B wins 7 seats with 40% of the statewide vote.

This isn't hypothetical. It's approximately what happened in Wisconsin, Pennsylvania, North Carolina, and Ohio following the 2010 census.[^ch4-1]
:::

## The Technology

Gerrymandering isn't new — the term dates to 1812, when Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry signed a redistricting plan that created a district shaped like a salamander. But modern gerrymandering is an entirely different beast, because the technology has transformed it from an art into a science.

Today's map-drawers have access to:

- **Block-level census data** — the exact population of every city block in America
- **Voter registration files** — the party affiliation (where registered) and voting history of every registered voter
- **Consumer data** — purchasing habits, magazine subscriptions, and other commercial data that predicts political preference with startling accuracy
- **GIS mapping software** — tools that can generate thousands of possible maps and evaluate each one for partisan advantage in seconds

::: deep-dive
### Algorithms of Control

In the 2020 redistricting cycle, both parties used algorithms that could evaluate *millions* of possible district configurations and rank them by partisan advantage. The software doesn't just draw favorable maps — it draws the *most* favorable map possible while still meeting legal requirements for equal population and contiguity.

One study by researchers at Duke University used the same algorithmic approach to generate thousands of random, non-partisan maps for North Carolina. They found that the state legislature's actual map was a statistical outlier — more favorable to Republicans than 99.999% of all possible maps that met legal requirements. The odds of producing such a map randomly were essentially zero. The map was engineered.[^ch4-2]

This is the core problem: modern redistricting isn't a political negotiation. It's a mathematical optimization problem, and the party in power has all the data, all the tools, and no obligation to be fair.
:::

## The Consequences

Gerrymandering doesn't just affect which party wins. It reshapes the entire incentive structure of American governance.

**It eliminates competitive elections.** After the 2020 redistricting cycle, analysts estimated that fewer than 40 of 435 House seats were genuinely competitive. The other 395 were effectively decided when the map was drawn — years before voters cast a single ballot.[^ch4-3]

**It polarizes Congress.** When districts are safe, the only election that matters is the primary. And primary electorates are extreme (see Chapter 1). Representatives in gerrymandered seats don't need to appeal to moderates or independents — they need to avoid being outflanked by someone more extreme in their own party. The result is a Congress populated by members who have no incentive to compromise and every incentive to perform for their base.

**It suppresses turnout.** Why vote when you know the outcome? In heavily gerrymandered states, turnout in non-competitive districts is consistently lower than in competitive ones. Voters aren't stupid — they understand when their vote doesn't matter, and they act accordingly.[^ch4-4]

**It insulates bad actors.** A representative in a safe seat can ignore constituent concerns, engage in scandal, or simply be bad at the job — and still win reelection. The district was drawn to make them safe, and short of a primary challenge (which rarely succeeds), they face no accountability.

::: callout
**The accountability gap:** In 2022, the average margin of victory in U.S. House races was over 30 percentage points. Thirty. That's not an election — that's a coronation. In most House districts, the incumbent could lose a quarter of their supporters and still win comfortably. This is not a system that produces representatives who fear the voters.
:::

## The Legal Landscape

Can't courts fix this?

They've tried. The results have been mixed — and recently, discouraging.

**Racial gerrymandering** — drawing districts to dilute minority voting power — is illegal under the Voting Rights Act. Courts have struck down racially gerrymandered maps in North Carolina, Virginia, Alabama, and elsewhere. But proving racial intent is difficult, and the line between racial and partisan gerrymandering is deliberately blurred by map-drawers who argue they were sorting by party, not race.

**Partisan gerrymandering** is a different story. In *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019), the Supreme Court ruled 5–4 that partisan gerrymandering claims are *non-justiciable* — meaning federal courts can't hear them at all. Chief Justice Roberts, writing for the majority, acknowledged that "excessive partisanship in districting leads to results that reasonably seem unjust" but concluded that there was no manageable standard for courts to apply.[^ch4-5]

::: deep-dive
### Justice Kagan's Dissent

Justice Elena Kagan's dissent in *Rucho* is one of the most powerful pieces of legal writing in recent Supreme Court history. She wrote:

> "For the first time ever, this Court refuses to remedy a constitutional violation because it thinks the task beyond judicial capabilities... The practices challenged in these cases imperil our system of government. Part of the Court's role in that system is to defend its foundations. None is more important than free and fair elections."

She argued that manageable standards *do* exist — the same mathematical tools used to engineer gerrymandered maps can detect them. Social scientists and mathematicians have developed multiple metrics for measuring partisan bias in maps, including the "efficiency gap," "mean-median difference," and "partisan symmetry" tests. The majority chose not to use them.[^ch4-6]

The dissent concluded: "Of all times to abandon the Court's duty to declare the law, this was not the one. The practices here at issue undermine the very foundations of democratic governance."
:::

With federal courts closed, the fight over gerrymandering has shifted to state courts (which can still hear partisan gerrymandering claims under state constitutions) and to ballot initiatives creating independent redistricting commissions — the subject of Chapter 9.

## The Human Cost

Behind the math and the legal arguments, gerrymandering has a human cost that's easy to overlook.

Consider what it means to live in a packed district. Your community has been deliberately concentrated into a single district to waste your voting power. You "win" your district overwhelmingly, but your neighbors in surrounding districts — people who share your values and concerns — have been cracked apart and submerged into districts where their voices are drowned out.

Or consider what it means to be a moderate in a safe district. Your representative answers to primary voters, not to you. They have no reason to hold town halls in your neighborhood, respond to your letters, or consider your perspective on legislation. You are politically invisible — not because you don't participate, but because the map was drawn to make your participation irrelevant.

This is what it feels like when your democracy is stolen — not through dramatic coups or voter intimidation, but through the quiet, technical manipulation of lines on a map.

::: callout
**The paradox of choice:** Americans can choose from 150 varieties of salad dressing at the grocery store but in most congressional elections face a predetermined outcome chosen by map-drawers years in advance. The system offers the *appearance* of choice — you can still vote — while eliminating its *substance*. This gap between democratic theater and democratic reality is gerrymandering's most corrosive legacy.
:::

## It Doesn't Have to Be This Way

Gerrymandering is not universal. Most democracies don't let politicians draw their own electoral maps. Many don't even use single-member districts.

Countries like Canada, Australia, and the UK use independent boundary commissions — nonpartisan bodies that draw districts based on population, geography, and community cohesion, not partisan advantage. Their maps aren't perfect, but they're not engineered for one team's benefit.

Several U.S. states have already adopted independent or bipartisan redistricting commissions: Arizona, California, Colorado, Michigan, and Virginia, among others. The results vary — some commissions have produced significantly fairer maps, while others have been undermined by partisan gamesmanship. But the trajectory is clear: voters want this power taken away from politicians.

Chapter 9 will explore what independent redistricting looks like in practice — what's worked, what hasn't, and how to design a commission that actually delivers fair maps. The tools exist. The precedents exist. The only thing missing is the political will to use them.

And the people who need to supply that will? That's us.
