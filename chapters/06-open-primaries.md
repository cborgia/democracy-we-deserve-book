# Chapter 6: Open Primaries

In 2022, Lisa Murkowski won reelection to the U.S. Senate from Alaska. That fact alone isn't remarkable. What's remarkable is *how* she won — and what it tells us about the power of opening up primary elections.

Murkowski is a moderate Republican. In 2010, she lost the Republican primary to a Tea Party challenger and only held her seat by winning a write-in campaign — one of the most difficult feats in American politics. For the next decade, she walked a tightrope: moderate enough to occasionally cross party lines, conservative enough to survive in a red state, and perpetually vulnerable to being primaried from the right.

Then Alaska changed the rules.

In 2020, Alaska voters approved a ballot measure replacing the traditional partisan primary with a **nonpartisan open primary**. All candidates — regardless of party — run in a single primary. The top four vote-getters advance to the general election, which uses ranked choice voting.

In 2022, Murkowski ran in this new system. She didn't have to survive a Republican primary dominated by the party's most extreme voters. She just had to be one of the four most popular candidates among *all* Alaskans. She advanced easily, won the general election, and was free to govern without the constant threat of a partisan primary challenge hanging over every vote.[^ch6-1]

That freedom — the freedom to represent all of your constituents, not just the most extreme members of your party — is what open primaries are designed to create.

## The Closed Primary Problem

To understand why open primaries matter, you need to understand what they replace.

In a **closed primary**, only registered members of a party can vote in that party's primary. If you're registered as an independent — as 43% of Americans are — you're locked out entirely. You don't get a say in which candidates appear on the general election ballot. By the time you're allowed to vote, your options have already been narrowed to two (or effectively one, in gerrymandered districts).

In a **semi-closed** or **semi-open primary**, independents can sometimes participate, but the rules vary wildly by state and often change between cycles. Some states require you to declare a party affiliation at the polling place. Others let you vote in either primary but not both. The confusion itself is a barrier.

In a **closed caucus** system (used by some states for presidential nominations), participation requires attending a multi-hour meeting at a specific time and place — effectively excluding anyone who works nights, has childcare constraints, or simply can't afford to spend a Tuesday evening at a school gymnasium.

::: callout
**The participation gap:** In the 2024 presidential primaries, approximately 35 million voters participated in Democratic primaries and 32 million in Republican primaries. That's roughly 67 million combined — out of approximately 240 million eligible voters. More than 170 million Americans had no voice in selecting the two people who would compete for the presidency.[^ch6-2]

In many states, they were *prohibited* from having a voice — not because they chose not to participate, but because the rules wouldn't let them.
:::

The closed primary system creates the exact incentive structure described in Chapters 1 and 2: a small, ideologically extreme electorate selects candidates who reflect that extremism, and the rest of the country is forced to choose between them.

## How Open Primaries Work

The term "open primary" covers several different systems, and the differences matter:

**Traditional open primary:** Any voter can vote in either party's primary, regardless of registration. This is better than closed primaries but still maintains separate party ballots and still produces two partisan nominees.

**Nonpartisan blanket primary (Top-two):** All candidates from all parties appear on a single ballot. The top two vote-getters advance to the general election, regardless of party. Used in California and Washington state since 2010 and 2008, respectively.

**Nonpartisan top-four primary:** Same concept, but the top four advance. Used in Alaska since 2022. The wider field reduces the "squeeze" effect and allows more ideological diversity in the general election.

**Nonpartisan top-five primary:** Similar to top-four. Nevada voters approved this system in 2024.

::: deep-dive
### The Alaska Model in Detail

Alaska's system — officially called the "Top-Four Open Primary" — works like this:

1. **Primary election:** All candidates appear on one ballot. No party labels are required (though candidates can list their party if they choose). All registered voters can participate. The top four vote-getters advance.

2. **General election:** The four advancing candidates compete using ranked choice voting. Voters rank them in order of preference. If no candidate receives a majority of first-choice votes, the last-place candidate is eliminated and their votes redistribute. This continues until one candidate has a majority.

The combination of an open primary with a multi-candidate general election fundamentally changes the incentive structure. Candidates don't need to win a partisan primary — they need broad appeal. They don't need to be the *most* popular with a narrow base — they need to avoid being the *least* popular among the full electorate.

The result is candidates who have a reason to reach across the aisle, appeal to independents, and govern for everyone — because their political survival depends on it.[^ch6-3]
:::

## What the Evidence Shows

Open primaries are relatively new at the state level, but the early evidence is encouraging.

**California's top-two primary** (adopted in 2010) has produced several notable effects:

- More moderate candidates winning in previously safe districts
- Occasional same-party general elections (two Democrats or two Republicans), forcing ideological differentiation within parties
- Increased competitiveness in districts that were previously uncontested
- Members of the California legislature scoring as more moderate on voting indices compared to pre-reform[^ch6-4]

**Washington state's top-two primary** (adopted in 2008) has shown similar patterns — more competitive races, more centrist candidates, and higher general election turnout in previously safe districts.

**Alaska's top-four primary** (first used in 2022) is still young, but its signature achievement is already clear: it enabled Lisa Murkowski and Mary Peltola (a moderate Democrat who won Alaska's at-large House seat) to win by appealing to the full electorate rather than surviving partisan gatekeeping.

::: callout
**The moderation effect:** A 2021 study of California's top-two primary found that state legislators elected under the new system were significantly more likely to vote across party lines and less likely to adhere to strict party-line positions. The effect was strongest in districts that had previously been safe for one party — exactly where the reform was designed to have impact.[^ch6-5]
:::

## The Opposition

Not everyone supports open primaries. The objections come from two directions — and they're revealing.

**Party leaders oppose them** because open primaries threaten party control. If any voter can participate in any primary, parties can't gatekeep their nominations. Party insiders, ideological activists, and major donors lose influence over candidate selection. This is, of course, precisely the point — but it means that the most powerful actors in the current system are incentivized to fight reform.

Both the Republican and Democratic parties have legally challenged open primary systems. In *California Democratic Party v. Jones* (2000), the Supreme Court struck down California's original blanket primary (where voters could vote in different parties' primaries for different offices) on the grounds that it violated parties' First Amendment right of association. The current top-two system survived legal challenge because it technically isn't a party primary at all — it's a nonpartisan qualifying round.[^ch6-6]

**Ideological purists on both sides oppose them** because open primaries dilute their influence. Progressive activists who've spent years building power within Democratic primaries don't want moderate or conservative voters diluting their candidate pool. Conservative activists feel the same way about their Republican primaries.

::: deep-dive
### The "Party Freedom" Argument

The most intellectually honest argument against open primaries is the freedom of association claim: parties are private organizations, and they should have the right to choose their own nominees through their own processes.

This argument has some merit in the abstract. But it collides with reality in two important ways:

**First**, primaries are publicly funded. Taxpayers pay hundreds of millions of dollars to administer primary elections. If parties want a private selection process, they're free to hold one — at their own expense. But the moment they use public infrastructure and public money, the public has a legitimate interest in the process being open to all.

**Second**, in a system where general elections are largely predetermined (due to gerrymandering and safe districts), the primary *is* the election for most Americans. Restricting access to the primary isn't protecting a private organization's rights — it's restricting access to the democratic process itself.

You can't claim to be pro-democracy while supporting a system that excludes 43% of voters from the only election that matters.[^ch6-7]
:::

## Beyond the Primary: The Ecosystem Effect

Open primaries don't just change who wins. They change how governance works.

When elected officials know they'll face the full electorate — not just their party's base — in the next primary, their behavior changes. They're more willing to compromise. More willing to work across party lines. More willing to take politically difficult votes that serve their constituents rather than their party.

This isn't speculation. Congressional scholars have documented that representatives from states with open primaries score as more moderate and more bipartisan than representatives from states with closed primaries, even after controlling for district partisanship.[^ch6-8]

The effect extends to legislative productivity. When members aren't terrified of primary challenges from their flank, they can actually legislate. They can negotiate. They can make the deals that democratic governance requires.

::: callout
**The "governing majority" effect:** In California, the top-two primary system has enabled occasional bipartisan coalitions in the state legislature — moderate Democrats and moderate Republicans working together to pass budgets, infrastructure bills, and other legislation that couldn't pass when both sides were locked into partisan positions by their primary electorates. This is governance as it's supposed to work — messy, compromise-driven, and responsive to the actual needs of constituents.
:::

## The Bigger Picture

Open primaries are not a silver bullet. In isolation, they don't solve gerrymandering, campaign finance corruption, or the two-party trap. But they're a critical piece of the puzzle — perhaps the piece with the most immediate, tangible impact on how elected officials behave.

Here's why: open primaries change the **incentive structure**. Every other reform in this book — score voting, proportional representation, independent redistricting, democracy dollars — also changes incentive structures. But open primaries do it at the chokepoint of the system: the moment when candidates are selected.

If the problem is that a small, extreme electorate picks candidates that don't represent the broader public (and it is), the solution is straightforward: let the broader public pick the candidates.

It's not complicated. It's not radical. It's the most basic democratic principle there is — that the people should choose their leaders. Open primaries simply extend that principle to the part of the process where it matters most.
