# Chapter 9: Independent Redistricting

In 2018, Michigan voters went to the polls and did something their state legislature had refused to do for decades: they took redistricting out of the hands of politicians.

The ballot measure — Proposal 2, also known as "Voters Not Politicians" — created an independent citizens' redistricting commission to draw congressional and state legislative district maps. It passed with 61% of the vote, winning in every single congressional district in the state, including deep-red rural districts and deep-blue urban ones.[^ch9-1]

The campaign was started by Katie Fahey, a 28-year-old who posted on Facebook: "I'd like to take on gerrymandering in Michigan. If you're interested in doing this as well, please let me know." That post led to a volunteer army of thousands, a successful petition drive, and one of the most significant democracy reforms in recent American history.

If that doesn't give you hope, nothing will.

## Why Politicians Can't Be Trusted with Maps

Chapter 4 explained *how* gerrymandering works. This chapter is about *who* draws the maps — and why that matters as much as the method.

In most states, redistricting is controlled by the state legislature. The party in power draws the maps. They use those maps to entrench their power. Then they use that power to draw the *next* set of maps. It's a self-perpetuating cycle — the political equivalent of a company writing its own regulations.

The conflict of interest is so obvious it barely needs explaining. And yet, as of 2024, state legislatures still controlled redistricting in the majority of states — roughly 30 of 50 for congressional maps and a similar number for state legislative maps.[^ch9-2]

::: callout
**The fox guarding the henhouse:** Imagine if judges could choose which cases they heard, prosecutors could pick their own juries, and CEOs could set their own salaries with no board oversight. That's the level of self-dealing built into legislative redistricting. The people who benefit most from gerrymandered maps are the same people who draw them. In no other area of American governance would we tolerate this kind of structural corruption.
:::

## Models of Independent Redistricting

Not all redistricting commissions are created equal. The details of how a commission is structured, who serves on it, and what criteria it uses determine whether it actually produces fair maps or just provides political cover for the same old manipulation.

### Fully Independent Citizen Commissions

**Example: Michigan, California, Colorado**

These commissions are composed of ordinary citizens — not politicians, lobbyists, or political operatives. Members are selected through an application and screening process designed to produce a body that's demographically and politically representative of the state.

Michigan's commission consists of 13 members: four who identify as Republican, four as Democratic, and five who are independent or affiliated with a minor party. Maps must be approved by a supermajority that includes members from each group — meaning no single faction can push through a partisan map.

::: deep-dive
### How Michigan Selects Commissioners

Michigan's selection process is designed to be resistant to gaming:

1. The Secretary of State mails applications to 10,000 randomly selected registered voters (like jury duty notices).
2. Any registered voter can also apply on their own.
3. A pool of qualified applicants is screened to remove anyone with conflicts of interest — current or recent elected officials, lobbyists, paid political operatives, party officers, and their immediate family members.
4. The applicant pool is narrowed to 200 semi-finalists through a stratified random selection process that ensures geographic, demographic, and partisan balance.
5. Legislative leaders from each party can strike up to 20 applicants each — a concession to political reality that gives parties some input while preventing them from controlling the outcome.
6. The final 13 commissioners are randomly drawn from the remaining pool.

The process is transparent, verifiable, and resistant to capture. It's not perfect — any human process has imperfections — but it's light-years ahead of letting the majority party in the legislature draw its own maps.[^ch9-3]
:::

### Bipartisan / Politician Commissions

**Example: Arizona, New Jersey**

These commissions include politicians or political appointees but are structured to require bipartisan agreement. Arizona's commission has two Republicans, two Democrats, and one independent chair. New Jersey's includes members appointed by legislative leaders from both parties.

**Strengths:** Ensures political expertise is at the table. Bipartisan requirement prevents one-party domination.

**Weaknesses:** Politicians and political appointees still control the process. "Bipartisan" can mean "both parties agree to protect incumbents" rather than "fair maps." The independent tiebreaker can become the most lobbied person in the state.

### Advisory Commissions

**Example: Iowa, New York (pre-2022)**

These bodies draw proposed maps, but the legislature retains final approval authority. Iowa's Legislative Services Agency draws maps using strict nonpartisan criteria — no political data, no incumbent addresses — and the legislature votes yes or no without amendment.

::: callout
**Iowa: The gold standard that almost was.** Iowa's system consistently produces some of the fairest maps in the country. Its simplicity is its strength: nonpartisan staff draw maps based on population, compactness, and county boundaries. No political data allowed. Period. The legislature can reject the first two proposals but must accept the third. In practice, the legislature has never rejected a map — the political cost of publicly refusing a nonpartisan map is too high. But the system's weakness is that it's a statute, not a constitutional amendment. The legislature could repeal it at any time.[^ch9-4]
:::

### Hybrid Approaches

**Example: Virginia, Montana**

Virginia's commission (adopted in 2020) includes eight legislators and eight citizens. It requires a supermajority including both legislators and citizens to approve maps. If the commission deadlocks, the Virginia Supreme Court draws the maps — which is exactly what happened in its first cycle in 2021.

## What Makes a Good Commission

After two decades of experimentation, researchers and reform advocates have identified the key design features that separate effective commissions from window dressing:

**1. True independence from legislators.** Commissions that include sitting legislators or are appointed entirely by legislative leaders tend to produce maps that protect incumbents. The most effective commissions exclude politicians and political operatives entirely.

**2. Transparent public process.** Maps should be drawn in public, with public hearings, public comment periods, and publicly available data. Backroom dealing is how partisan maps get made. Sunlight is the best disinfectant.

**3. Clear, ranked criteria.** The commission should follow explicit criteria — equal population, compliance with the Voting Rights Act, geographic contiguity, compactness, preservation of communities of interest, and competitiveness — ranked in order of priority. Without clear criteria, commissioners are left to make subjective judgments that can be influenced by partisan considerations.

::: deep-dive
### The Criteria Debate

What criteria should redistricting commissions prioritize? This is where the technical gets political.

**Equal population** is constitutionally required. **Voting Rights Act compliance** is legally required. Beyond those, the ranking gets contested:

**Compactness** — Districts should be geographically compact, avoiding the bizarre tentacle shapes that characterize gerrymandered maps. But strict compactness can split communities that are geographically spread out.

**Preservation of communities of interest** — Districts should keep together communities that share economic, social, or cultural ties. But defining "community of interest" is inherently subjective and can be manipulated.

**Competitiveness** — Districts should be drawn to maximize electoral competition. But prioritizing competitiveness can conflict with preserving communities and can produce districts that don't reflect natural geographic patterns.

**Proportionality** — The statewide map should produce a partisan distribution of seats roughly proportional to the statewide partisan vote. This is outcome-oriented rather than process-oriented, which makes some reformers uncomfortable — but it's arguably the most direct measure of fairness.

There's no perfect ranking. But having *any* explicit, transparent criteria — applied consistently and publicly — is infinitely better than the current system in most states, where the criteria are "maximize our party's advantage" and the process is "closed-door meetings with consultants."[^ch9-5]
:::

**4. Supermajority or cross-partisan approval.** Maps should require approval from commissioners across partisan lines. Simple majority approval allows one faction to dominate. Michigan's requirement that final maps be approved by a majority that includes at least two members from each partisan group is a strong model.

**5. Legal durability.** Commissions established by statute can be repealed by the legislature. Commissions established by constitutional amendment are far harder to undo. Every major successful redistricting reform in recent years — Michigan, California, Colorado — has been enacted through constitutional ballot initiatives, not legislative action.

## The Results So Far

How have independent commissions actually performed? The evidence is mixed but mostly encouraging.

**California** adopted its Citizens Redistricting Commission in 2010. The maps it produced were dramatically more competitive than the previous legislature-drawn maps. The number of competitive House seats in California increased from roughly 2 to 10. The commission process was praised by good-government groups and criticized by both parties — which is probably the best indicator of fairness.[^ch9-6]

**Arizona** adopted its independent commission in 2000. The commission has faced legal challenges and political attacks from both parties in every cycle, which — again — suggests it's threatening the status quo. The Supreme Court upheld the commission's constitutionality in *Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Commission* (2015), a critical precedent for citizen-led redistricting reform.[^ch9-7]

**Michigan** used its citizen commission for the first time in 2021. The process was messy — commissioners struggled with the complexity of the task, advocacy groups pushed for favorable interpretations of "community of interest," and the maps were challenged in court. But the resulting maps were measurably fairer than the previous legislature-drawn maps, producing more competitive districts and better proportional representation.

::: callout
**Perfection isn't the standard.** Independent commissions sometimes produce imperfect maps. Commissioners are human, the task is genuinely complex, and no process eliminates all political considerations. The question isn't "Are commission-drawn maps perfect?" It's "Are they better than maps drawn by self-interested politicians?" The answer, consistently and overwhelmingly, is yes.
:::

## The Opposition Playbook

Politicians whose power depends on gerrymandered maps don't give that power up quietly. The playbook for fighting redistricting reform is well-established:

**Legal challenges.** Every major commission has been sued, usually by the party that would have controlled redistricting under the old system. Some challenges have merit (implementation details can have legal vulnerabilities). Many are delay tactics.

**Undermining from within.** In states where commissions were established by the legislature rather than ballot initiative, lawmakers have repeatedly tried to weaken them — reducing funding, changing appointment rules, or limiting the criteria commissions can use.

**False equivalence.** "Both parties gerrymander" is technically true and strategically deployed to create the impression that the problem is unsolvable. The goal is to make voters cynical enough to stop pushing for reform.

**Co-option.** In some states, parties have tried to pack commissions with sympathetic members by gaming the selection process. Michigan's disqualification criteria and random selection process were specifically designed to resist this.

## The Scale of the Opportunity

Here's the math that should motivate every democracy reformer:

Congressional maps are redrawn every 10 years, after each census. The next redistricting cycle begins in 2031. Between now and then, reform advocates have the opportunity to establish independent commissions in dozens of states through ballot initiatives and (where necessary) legislative action.

If 10-15 more states adopted independent redistricting commissions before the 2030 census, the combined effect would be transformative. The number of genuinely competitive House seats could double or triple. The incentive structure for hundreds of elected officials would shift from "survive the primary" to "win the general" — which means governing for the full electorate rather than performing for the base.

::: deep-dive
### The Ballot Initiative Advantage

Redistricting reform has a built-in advantage that most democracy reforms don't: **ballot initiatives bypass the legislature.** In the 24 states that allow citizen-initiated ballot measures, voters can put redistricting reform directly on the ballot without any support from the politicians it threatens.

This is exactly how Michigan, California, Colorado, and several other states adopted their commissions. The Voters Not Politicians campaign in Michigan collected over 425,000 signatures — more than any ballot initiative in state history at that time — with an all-volunteer effort.

The lesson: legislators will never vote to give up the power to draw their own maps. But voters will vote to take that power away. The ballot initiative is the mechanism, and the evidence from states that have used it is clear: **when citizens vote on redistricting reform, reform wins.** Every single statewide redistricting ballot initiative has passed since 2010.[^ch9-8]
:::

## Making It Work in Your State

If you live in a state that allows ballot initiatives and doesn't have an independent redistricting commission, the blueprint exists:

1. **Build a broad coalition.** Redistricting reform has natural allies across the political spectrum — good-government groups, civil rights organizations, business associations, and voters who are simply tired of predetermined elections. Michigan's campaign included Republicans, Democrats, and independents.

2. **Design the commission carefully.** Study what's worked and what hasn't. Exclude politicians and lobbyists. Use random selection from a large applicant pool. Require cross-partisan approval of final maps. Enshrine it in the state constitution, not just statute.

3. **Collect signatures early and widely.** Signature drives build awareness and volunteer networks simultaneously. Start at least 18 months before the filing deadline.

4. **Prepare for opposition.** The campaign against reform will be well-funded and will deploy every tool in the playbook — legal challenges, media campaigns, and misinformation. Plan for it.

5. **Win.** And then defend your win in the courts, because the lawsuits will come.

The fight for fair maps isn't glamorous. It doesn't generate viral moments or trending hashtags. But it may be the single most impactful structural reform available to citizens — because when you change who draws the maps, you change who wins the elections, which changes who governs, which changes everything.
