# Chapter 11: States Leading the Way

The conventional wisdom about democracy reform goes like this: the system is too broken to fix, the people in power will never give up that power, and nothing will change until some catastrophic failure forces it.

The conventional wisdom is wrong.

Across the country — in red states, blue states, and purple states — citizens are rewriting the rules of democracy. Not waiting for Congress. Not asking permission from party leaders. They're using ballot initiatives, grassroots organizing, and sheer persistence to implement the exact reforms described in this book.

And it's working.

This chapter profiles the states and cities at the frontier of democracy reform. These aren't theoretical proposals. They're real experiments, with real results, happening right now.

## Alaska: The Full Package

Alaska is the closest thing America has to a laboratory for comprehensive democracy reform.

In 2020, Alaska voters approved Ballot Measure 2, which combined two reforms into a single package: a **nonpartisan top-four open primary** and **ranked choice voting** in the general election. The measure passed with 50.55% of the vote — razor-thin, and against fierce opposition from both major parties.[^ch11-1]

The results were immediate.

**The 2022 elections** were Alaska's first under the new system, and they produced outcomes that would have been impossible under the old rules:

- **Lisa Murkowski** won reelection to the Senate. Under the old closed Republican primary, she almost certainly would have lost to a Trump-endorsed challenger — as she had in 2010, when she survived only through a write-in campaign. The open primary let all Alaskans participate, and the broader electorate chose the candidate with the widest appeal.

- **Mary Peltola** won Alaska's sole House seat — the first Democrat to hold it in nearly 50 years, and the first Alaska Native ever elected to Congress. In a traditional closed primary, she wouldn't have made it past the Democratic primary (Alaska is deeply Republican). In the open primary, she was one of the top four. In the ranked choice general, she won by being the second choice of enough voters across party lines.

- **Bipartisan governance** followed. Murkowski and Peltola — a Republican and a Democrat — found common ground on Alaska-specific issues like fisheries, energy, and tribal sovereignty. Neither owed their election to party leadership. Both owed it to the full Alaska electorate.

::: deep-dive
### What Alaska's System Actually Changed

The behavioral shift among candidates was as significant as the electoral results.

Under the old system, candidates ran hard to the right (Republicans) or hard to the left (Democrats) to survive their respective primaries. Under the new system, candidates needed broad appeal from the start. Campaign rhetoric shifted. Attack ads decreased. Candidates began attending community events outside their partisan base.

Scott Kendall, the attorney who helped draft Ballot Measure 2, described the logic: "When your political survival depends on being acceptable to the majority of voters — not just the most extreme 20% — you govern differently. You show up differently. You *are* different."

Not everyone is happy. The Alaska Republican Party sued (unsuccessfully) to block the system. Former Governor Sarah Palin blamed ranked choice voting for her loss in the 2022 House special election — though analysis showed she would have lost under any voting system, because more Alaskans preferred Peltola.[^ch11-2]

The system has survived its first tests. Alaska will continue refining it, and the rest of the country is watching.
:::

## California: The Long Game

California's democracy reforms didn't happen overnight. They were built over more than a decade through a series of ballot initiatives, each addressing a different piece of the puzzle.

**2008: Top-two primary (Proposition 14).** California replaced its closed partisan primaries with a nonpartisan top-two system. All candidates appear on one ballot. The top two advance to the general election, regardless of party. The result: occasional same-party general elections that force ideological differentiation, more moderate candidates in previously safe districts, and a measurably less polarized state legislature.

**2010: Citizens Redistricting Commission (Proposition 20, building on 2008's Proposition 11).** California took redistricting out of the legislature's hands and gave it to an independent citizen commission. The commission drew dramatically more competitive maps. The number of competitive congressional seats roughly tripled.

**2010: Majority-vote budget (Proposition 25).** California changed its budget approval threshold from a two-thirds supermajority to a simple majority. This ended decades of budget gridlock caused by a small minority blocking legislation.

**2016: Modified term limits (Proposition 28, originally passed in 2012).** California loosened its overly strict term limits, allowing members to serve 12 years in either chamber rather than 6 in the Assembly and 8 in the Senate. This preserved the anti-entrenchment benefit while addressing the institutional knowledge problem.

::: callout
**The compound effect:** No single California reform was transformative on its own. But combined, they fundamentally changed how the state is governed. California's legislature — once famously dysfunctional, with budget crises, extreme partisanship, and legislative paralysis — became markedly more functional after the reforms. Bills get passed. Budgets get approved on time. Cross-party coalitions form. The state still has plenty of problems, but its *governance* has improved measurably.

This is the model. Not one silver bullet, but a series of structural changes that reinforce each other.[^ch11-3]
:::

## Michigan: People Power

Michigan's story is about what happens when ordinary citizens decide they've had enough.

In 2018, Katie Fahey — a 28-year-old with no political experience — launched "Voters Not Politicians" with a Facebook post. Within months, the all-volunteer campaign had collected over 425,000 signatures (the most of any ballot initiative in Michigan history at that time) and put an independent redistricting commission on the ballot.

Proposal 2 passed with 61% of the vote. It won in every congressional district — urban and rural, liberal and conservative. The message was clear: across partisan lines, voters agreed that politicians shouldn't draw their own maps.

The commission drew its first maps in 2021 for the 2022 elections. The process was public, messy, and imperfect — commissioners struggled with the complexity of balancing multiple criteria, and advocacy groups pushed hard for favorable interpretations. But the resulting maps were dramatically fairer than the previous legislature-drawn maps.

::: deep-dive
### Michigan Before and After

The contrast is stark:

**Before (2012-2020 maps, drawn by Republican legislature):**
- Republicans won 9-5 or 10-4 House seat advantages in a state where the statewide popular vote was roughly 50-50
- Zero competitive state legislative districts in many cycles
- Michigan was rated one of the most gerrymandered states in the country

**After (2022 maps, drawn by citizen commission):**
- House delegation shifted closer to proportional: 7-6 split
- Multiple competitive state legislative seats
- Democrats won the state legislature for the first time in 40 years — not because the maps were drawn to favor them, but because fair maps in a genuinely 50-50 state produce competitive elections

The maps weren't perfect. Some districts were criticized for splitting communities. The commission's internal dynamics were occasionally contentious. But the fundamental outcome — maps drawn for the public, in public, by the public — was exactly what voters had demanded.[^ch11-4]
:::

## Maine: First in Ranked Choice

Maine holds the distinction of being the first state to use ranked choice voting for federal elections.

The path was characteristically New England: stubborn, independent, and propelled by a specific grievance.

In both 2010 and 2014, Maine's governor was elected with less than 40% of the vote in three-way races. Paul LePage — a deeply polarizing figure — won with 37.6% in 2010 and 48.2% in 2014, both times benefiting from vote-splitting between the Democrat and a strong independent candidate. A majority of Maine voters preferred someone else. Twice.

In 2016, Maine voters approved ranked choice voting by ballot initiative. The legislature tried to delay and weaken it. Supporters fought back with a people's veto. In 2018, Maine used RCV for federal races — the first state in the nation to do so.

The 2018 2nd Congressional District race provided a textbook demonstration: the initial count showed the Republican incumbent leading, but after RCV tabulation transferred votes from the eliminated independent and third-party candidates, the Democratic challenger won. The system worked exactly as designed — the candidate with the broadest support prevailed.[^ch11-5]

::: callout
**Maine's lesson for reformers:** The establishment will fight. The legislature tried to repeal RCV, delay it, and restrict it to federal races only. The governor publicly opposed it. The state Republican Party sued. And the people of Maine beat them all — through a people's veto, grassroots organizing, and the courts. Reform is possible, but it requires sustained effort against entrenched opposition.
:::

## Colorado: The Quiet Revolution

While Alaska and Maine grabbed headlines, Colorado quietly became one of the most reformed states in the country.

In 2018, Colorado voters approved two ballot measures simultaneously:

**Amendment Y** created an independent redistricting commission for congressional maps.
**Amendment Z** created a parallel commission for state legislative maps.

Both passed with over 70% of the vote — overwhelming mandates for reform.[^ch11-6]

Colorado's commissions drew their first maps in 2021. The process was notably smoother than Michigan's — partly because Colorado's measures were drafted with more detailed criteria, partly because the political culture was more conducive to compromise. The resulting maps were praised by independent analysts as among the fairest in the nation.

But Colorado didn't stop at redistricting.

**Proposition 108 (2016)** opened Colorado's primaries to unaffiliated voters — the state's largest voting bloc. Independent voters could now participate in either party's primary without registering as a member. Turnout in primaries increased significantly, and the candidates who emerged were measurably more moderate.

Combined with the redistricting commissions, Colorado's reforms have produced a state where elections are competitive, representatives face real accountability, and the political culture is noticeably less toxic than the national average.

## Local Innovation

Some of the most interesting democracy experiments are happening at the city and county level — laboratories of democracy in the truest sense.

**St. Louis, Missouri** adopted approval voting for its primary elections in 2020 — becoming the first major U.S. city to use a non-plurality voting method citywide. Under approval voting, voters can vote for as many candidates as they approve of. The candidate with the most votes wins. It's even simpler than ranked choice voting and produces similar anti-spoiler effects.[^ch11-7]

**Cambridge, Massachusetts** has used single transferable vote (a proportional representation method) for city council elections since 1941 — making it the longest-running proportional representation system in the United States. Cambridge consistently elects one of the most diverse city councils in the country.

**Seattle, Washington** implemented democracy vouchers in 2017 (profiled in Chapter 8), transforming the city's campaign finance landscape.

**Portland, Oregon** voters approved a new city government structure in 2022 that includes multi-member city council districts elected by single transferable vote — the first major U.S. city to adopt a proportional representation method in decades.

::: deep-dive
### The Local-to-State Pipeline

Almost every major state-level democracy reform started at the local level:

- Ranked choice voting was used in dozens of cities before Maine and Alaska adopted it statewide.
- Open primaries were tested in California and Washington before Alaska implemented its top-four version.
- Democracy vouchers started in Seattle before being considered at the state and federal level.
- Proportional representation in Cambridge has provided decades of evidence for national reform proposals.

Local adoption serves three functions: it **demonstrates** that the reform works in practice, it **builds** a network of voters and advocates who've experienced it firsthand, and it **normalizes** the concept so that statewide campaigns don't sound radical.

If you want to change how America votes, start with your city council. The path to national reform runs through local government.[^ch11-8]
:::

## The Map of Reform

As of 2025, the landscape of democracy reform looks like this:

**Ranked choice voting:** Used statewide in Alaska and Maine; used in over 50 cities and counties including New York City, Minneapolis, and San Francisco.

**Open primaries:** Nonpartisan top-two in California and Washington; top-four in Alaska; top-five approved in Nevada (2024).

**Independent redistricting:** Fully independent citizen commissions in Michigan, California, and Colorado; bipartisan or advisory commissions in Arizona, Iowa, Virginia, and several others.

**Democracy vouchers/public financing:** Democracy vouchers in Seattle and Oakland; public matching in New York City; various public financing programs in Connecticut, Maine, and Arizona.

**Alternative voting methods:** Approval voting in St. Louis and Fargo; STV in Cambridge; score voting in several smaller jurisdictions and organizational elections.

::: callout
**The momentum is real.** In 2010, only a handful of cities used ranked choice voting and no state had an independent citizen redistricting commission created by ballot initiative. By 2025, the reform landscape had expanded dramatically across all categories. Every cycle brings new ballot measures, new adoptions, and new evidence. The question isn't whether these reforms are spreading — it's how fast.
:::

## Lessons from the States

What can we learn from the states that have successfully reformed their democratic systems?

**1. Ballot initiatives are the most reliable path.** Legislators rarely reform themselves. In every major success story — Alaska, Michigan, California, Colorado, Maine — reform was driven by citizens through the ballot initiative process. States without ballot initiatives face a harder path but can still pursue reform through legislative lobbying and judicial challenges.

**2. Coalition breadth matters.** The most successful campaigns built explicitly nonpartisan coalitions. Michigan's Voters Not Politicians included Republican, Democratic, and independent supporters. Alaska's Ballot Measure 2 was backed by a diverse coalition of business leaders, native organizations, and good-government groups. Reform that's perceived as benefiting one party over another will face existential opposition.

**3. Combination beats isolation.** The most transformed states — Alaska, California, Colorado — didn't adopt one reform. They adopted several, often over multiple election cycles. Each reform reinforces the others: open primaries are more effective with independent redistricting; redistricting reform is more durable when the legislature isn't controlled by gerrymandered incumbents.

**4. Expect opposition and plan for it.** Every successful reform has been challenged in court, attacked by political parties, and subjected to misinformation campaigns. The organizations that succeed are the ones that plan for this from the start — budgeting for legal defense, building rapid-response communications, and maintaining volunteer networks beyond election day.

**5. Results are the best argument.** The single most effective advocacy tool for democracy reform is a working example. Once voters in one city or state experience a reformed system, they become ambassadors. And voters in neighboring jurisdictions start asking: why can't we have that here?
