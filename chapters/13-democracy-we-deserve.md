# Chapter 13: The Democracy We Deserve

There's a saying — usually attributed to Alexis de Tocqueville, though he probably never said it — that "in a democracy, the people get the government they deserve."

It's meant as a rebuke. You voted for these people. You tolerated this system. You got what was coming.

But there's another way to read it. A more generous way. A way that isn't about blame but about potential:

**We get the democracy we build.**

If we build a system with winner-take-all elections, we get two parties. If we build a system with closed primaries, we get extreme candidates. If we let politicians draw their own maps, we get gerrymandered districts. If we let money flow unchecked into elections, we get a government that answers to donors instead of voters.

None of this is destiny. All of it is design. And design can be changed.

## What We've Learned

This book has covered a lot of ground. Let's take a step back and see the full picture.

**The problem is structural, not moral.** American democracy isn't failing because Americans are bad people, bad voters, or bad citizens. It's failing because the machinery — the voting systems, the primary rules, the district boundaries, the campaign finance framework — produces bad outcomes regardless of the people operating it. Good people in a bad system produce bad results. Fix the system and the same people produce better results.

**The solutions exist and they work.** Every reform in this book is already operating somewhere — in an American city, an American state, or a democracy abroad. Score voting, open primaries, proportional representation, independent redistricting, democracy dollars — these aren't theoretical proposals from academic papers. They're tested systems with measurable results.

**The reforms reinforce each other.** No single reform is sufficient. But in combination, they dismantle the structural foundations of democratic dysfunction:

::: callout
**The reform stack:**
- **Score voting** eliminates the spoiler effect and makes third parties viable
- **Open primaries** end partisan gatekeeping and incentivize broad appeal
- **Independent redistricting** prevents map manipulation and creates competitive elections
- **Proportional representation** ensures that every vote translates to representation
- **Democracy dollars** dilute the influence of big money and diversify the donor pool
- **Term limits and accountability** prevent entrenchment and demand performance

Each reform makes the others more effective. Open primaries matter more when districts are fairly drawn. Fair districts matter more when the voting system rewards broad appeal. Broad-appeal candidates matter more when they're accountable to the full electorate rather than a handful of donors. The system works as a system.
:::

**Citizens are the mechanism of change.** In every success story — Alaska, Michigan, Maine, California, Colorado — reform was driven by citizens, not politicians. Ballot initiatives, grassroots organizing, and persistent advocacy overcame institutional resistance. The people who benefit from the current system will never voluntarily reform it. Change comes from outside.

## The Vision

Imagine, for a moment, what American democracy could look like.

**Election day.** You walk into a polling station and see a ballot with six candidates for your congressional district — not two. A fiscal conservative who supports climate action. A progressive who's strong on border security. A centrist focused on housing affordability. A libertarian. A democratic socialist. An independent with a background in education.

You score each one on a scale of 0 to 5. You don't have to pick sides or hold your nose. You express your actual preferences — all of them. When the results come in, the candidate with the broadest support wins. No spoilers. No wasted votes. No "lesser of two evils."

**Congress.** Representatives arrive knowing they were elected by — and are accountable to — the full range of voters in their district. Not just primary voters. Not just donors. Everyone. They form coalitions based on policy, not party. They negotiate in good faith because their political survival depends on results, not performance.

Five or six parties hold seats. The governing coalition shifts issue by issue. A center-right block passes a tax reform bill on Monday. A center-left block passes a healthcare expansion on Thursday. Both bills have genuine majority support. Neither is extreme. Neither was held hostage by partisan leadership.

**Money.** Every voter has $100 in democracy dollars. Candidates raise competitive funding from ordinary citizens. Big donors still contribute, but their money is diluted in an ocean of people-powered funding. Lobbyists still lobby, but legislators don't depend on them for reelection. The financial incentive to listen to constituents outweighs the financial incentive to listen to K Street.

**Accountability.** Representatives serve a maximum of 12 years. They're evaluated not just by party loyalty scores but by effectiveness metrics — bills passed, bipartisan cooperation, constituent engagement. An independent ethics body has real enforcement power. Financial disclosures are real-time and publicly accessible. When a representative fails, voters have the information and the competitive elections to replace them.

::: deep-dive
### Is This Utopian?

Every element of the scenario above exists somewhere in the world right now.

Score voting is used in organizational elections worldwide. Multi-party legislatures govern dozens of countries. Coalition governance is the norm in Northern Europe, Scandinavia, and much of the developed world. Democracy vouchers operate in Seattle. Independent redistricting commissions draw maps in multiple states. Term limits govern most American state legislatures.

None of this requires inventing anything new. It requires assembling existing, proven components into a coherent system. The only thing that's "utopian" about this vision is the idea that Americans would choose to implement it.

And given the polling — where every individual reform enjoys majority support — maybe even that isn't so utopian.[^ch13-1]
:::

## The Resistance

Let's be honest about what stands in the way.

**Incumbents.** People in power generally prefer the system that put them there. Asking Congress to reform itself is asking turkeys to vote for Thanksgiving. This is why ballot initiatives — which bypass the legislature — are the most reliable path. But they're only available in 24 states.

**Parties.** Both the Republican and Democratic parties are private organizations whose primary interest is maintaining their duopoly. They will oppose reforms that threaten their structural advantage — even when those reforms have overwhelming public support. Expect lawsuits, lobbying, and well-funded opposition campaigns.

**Inertia.** The most powerful force in American politics isn't money or partisanship. It's the deeply held assumption that the current system is the only possible system. "That's just how it works." "Third parties can't win." "Nothing will change." These beliefs are self-fulfilling prophecies — and they dissolve the moment you show people an alternative.

**Complexity.** Democracy reform requires explaining systems that most people have never heard of. Score voting, proportional representation, STV — these concepts aren't difficult, but they're unfamiliar. Education is the essential precondition for every other step.

::: callout
**The asymmetry of motivation:** The people who benefit from the current system — party insiders, gerrymandering beneficiaries, major donors — have concentrated, intense motivation to defend it. The people who would benefit from reform — the vast majority of voters — have diffuse, mild motivation to change it. This asymmetry is the fundamental challenge of democracy reform. Overcoming it requires making the case so clearly and so repeatedly that the diffuse majority becomes an organized force.

This book is an attempt to contribute to that clarity.
:::

## The Historical Moment

We've been here before.

In the early 1900s, American democracy was captured by political machines, corporate monopolies, and entrenched corruption. The system seemed impervious to change. And then — driven by muckraking journalists, grassroots activists, and a public that had simply had enough — the Progressive Era produced a cascade of structural reforms: direct election of senators (17th Amendment), women's suffrage (19th Amendment), ballot initiatives and referendums, civil service reform, and antitrust legislation.

In the 1960s, the civil rights movement faced equally entrenched opposition and produced the Voting Rights Act, the 24th Amendment (banning poll taxes), and a fundamental expansion of who counts as "the people" in American democracy.

Every generation faces a moment when the gap between democratic ideals and democratic reality becomes intolerable. **This is ours.**

The tools are better than they've ever been. The evidence base is stronger. The reform organizations are more sophisticated. The public support is overwhelming. What's missing is the collective decision to act.

## The Choice

Democracy reform is not a spectator sport. It doesn't happen because someone writes a book, passes a law, or wins an election. It happens because enough ordinary people decide — individually and then collectively — that the current system is unacceptable and that they're willing to do the work of building something better.

That work is unglamorous. It's collecting signatures at farmers' markets. It's attending city council meetings. It's knocking on doors to explain what a redistricting commission is. It's donating $25 to a reform campaign. It's voting in every primary, every local election, every school board race.

It's the most important work a citizen can do.

### A Personal Note

If you've read this far, you know more about structural democracy reform than 95% of Americans. That knowledge carries a responsibility — not a heavy or burdensome one, but a real one.

Talk about this. Not at Thanksgiving dinner (unless you enjoy conflict), but in the quiet conversations that actually change minds. Share what you've learned with one person. Support one reform effort. Vote in one election you would have skipped.

The system changes one person at a time, one city at a time, one state at a time. The pace feels slow until you look back and realize how far the movement has come in just 15 years.

The democracy we deserve isn't the democracy we have. It's the democracy we're willing to fight for. And the fight is winnable — if enough of us show up.
:::

The title of this book is a challenge, not a diagnosis.

We don't yet have the democracy we deserve. But we can. And whether we build it — or accept the broken version we inherited — is the defining choice of this generation.

Choose to build.
