# Appendix: Resources & Organizations {.unnumbered}

This appendix is a practical reference — organizations to support, books to read, tools to use, and actions to take. It's designed to be a starting point, not an exhaustive list. The democracy reform space is growing fast; check these organizations' websites for the most current information.

## Organizations

### Voting Reform

**FairVote**
fairvote.org
The leading organization advocating for ranked choice voting and proportional representation. Extensive research library, tracking of RCV adoption, and support for local campaigns.

**The Center for Election Science**
electionscience.org
Advocates for approval voting and score voting. Research-driven approach to voting method reform. Provided technical support for St. Louis and Fargo's adoption of approval voting.

**Equal Vote Coalition**
equal.vote
Advocates for STAR voting (Score Then Automatic Runoff). Based in Oregon, where STAR voting has been used in local party elections.

**Score Voting Advocates**
scorevoting.net
Educational resources and advocacy for score/range voting systems.

### Open Primaries & Electoral Competition

**Unite America**
uniteamerica.org
Nonpartisan organization supporting open primaries, independent redistricting, and other structural reforms. Funds reform campaigns and candidates. Published influential research on the "Primary Problem."

**Open Primaries**
openprimaries.org
Single-issue organization focused on opening primary elections to all voters regardless of party affiliation. State-by-state tracking and campaign support.

### Redistricting

**Common Cause**
commoncause.org
One of the oldest democracy reform organizations. Active in redistricting reform, voting rights, campaign finance, and ethics. State chapters in most states.

**Campaign Legal Center**
campaignlegal.org
Provides legal expertise on redistricting, campaign finance, and voting rights. Frequently involved in redistricting litigation.

**Princeton Gerrymandering Project**
gerrymander.princeton.edu
Academic project that grades redistricting maps for partisan fairness using quantitative metrics. Provides expert testimony in redistricting cases.

**All About Redistricting**
redistricting.lls.edu
Loyola Law School resource maintained by Professor Justin Levitt. Comprehensive, state-by-state guide to redistricting rules and processes.

### Campaign Finance

**OpenSecrets (Center for Responsive Politics)**
opensecrets.org
The definitive source for campaign finance data. Tracks contributions, lobbying, revolving door employment, and outside spending for every federal election.

**Issue One**
issueone.org
Bipartisan organization focused on reducing the influence of money in politics. Produces the "Fix the System" agenda and tracks dark money spending.

**Represent.Us**
represent.us
Anti-corruption organization supporting a range of reforms including ranked choice voting, independent redistricting, and campaign finance transparency. Focuses on state and local "Anti-Corruption Acts."

**Every Voice (now part of End Citizens United)**
endcitizensunited.org
Advocates for campaign finance reform and supports candidates who pledge to reform the system.

### Accountability & Transparency

**Transparency International — U.S. Office**
us.transparency.org
Anti-corruption research and advocacy. Publishes the Corruption Perceptions Index and tracks lobbying and ethics compliance.

**GovTrack**
govtrack.us
Free tool for tracking congressional legislation, votes, and member activity. Useful for evaluating representative performance.

**ProPublica — Represent**
projects.propublica.org/represent
Congressional voting record tracker with bill details and member profiles.

**Ballotpedia**
ballotpedia.org
Comprehensive encyclopedia of American politics — elections, ballot measures, candidates, and government. Essential reference for any reform campaign.

### Broad Coalition Organizations

**Bridge Alliance**
bridgealliance.us
Network of over 100 organizations working on democracy reform, civic engagement, and cross-partisan dialogue.

**Democracy Found**
democracyfound.org
Nonpartisan organization that helped pass Alaska's Ballot Measure 2. Provides consulting and support for other states pursuing similar reforms.

**Voters Not Politicians**
votersnotpoliticians.com
Michigan-based organization that passed the state's independent redistricting commission. Model for citizen-led redistricting campaigns.

## Books

### Essential Reading

- **Gehl, K.M. and Porter, M.E.** *The Politics Industry: How Political Innovation Can Break Partisan Gridlock and Save Our Democracy.* Harvard Business Review Press, 2020. — The most influential recent book on structural democracy reform. Applies business strategy frameworks to political dysfunction. Directly inspired Alaska's reform.

- **Drutman, L.** *Breaking the Two-Party Doom Loop: The Case for Multiparty Democracy in America.* Oxford University Press, 2020. — Comprehensive argument for proportional representation and multi-party democracy in the U.S.

- **Klein, E.** *Why We're Polarized.* Simon & Schuster, 2020. — How identity, media, and structural incentives drive polarization. Essential context for understanding why reform is needed.

- **Lijphart, A.** *Patterns of Democracy: Government Forms and Performance in Thirty-Six Countries.* Yale University Press, 2012. — The academic foundation for comparing democratic systems. Dense but definitive.

- **Hasen, R.L.** *Plutocrats United: Campaign Money, the Supreme Court, and the Distortion of American Elections.* Yale University Press, 2016. — Campaign finance law and reform after Citizens United.

### Additional Reading

- **Mason, L.** *Uncivil Agreement: How Politics Became Our Identity.* University of Chicago Press, 2018. — How partisan sorting reshapes political behavior.

- **Levitsky, S. and Ziblatt, D.** *How Democracies Die.* Crown, 2018. — Comparative analysis of democratic erosion, with lessons for the U.S.

- **Sitaraman, G.** *The Great Democracy: How to Fix Our Politics, Unrig the Economy, and Unite a Divided Country.* Basic Books, 2019. — Broader reform agenda including economic democracy.

- **Cain, B.E.** *Democracy More or Less: America's Political Reform Quandary.* Cambridge University Press, 2014. — Thoughtful analysis of tradeoffs in democracy reform.

- **Rosenstone, S.J., Behr, R.L., and Lazarus, E.H.** *Third Parties in America.* Princeton University Press, 1996. — History of third-party movements and why they fail under winner-take-all.

## Glossary

**Approval voting.** A voting method where voters can vote for (approve of) as many candidates as they like. The candidate with the most approvals wins.

**Blanket primary.** A primary election where all candidates from all parties appear on one ballot. Voters can vote for one candidate per office. The top vote-getters advance to the general election.

**Closed primary.** A primary election in which only voters registered with a specific party can vote in that party's primary.

**Condorcet winner.** A candidate who would defeat every other candidate in a head-to-head matchup. Considered the strongest measure of majority preference.

**Cracking.** A gerrymandering technique that splits a group of voters across multiple districts to prevent them from forming a majority in any single district.

**Democracy dollars / democracy vouchers.** Public funds distributed to every registered voter for the purpose of donating to political campaigns.

**Duverger's Law.** The observation that winner-take-all (plurality) voting systems tend to produce two-party systems.

**Efficiency gap.** A metric for measuring partisan gerrymandering based on the number of "wasted votes" — votes cast for losing candidates or surplus votes for winning candidates beyond what was needed to win.

**First-past-the-post (FPTP).** A voting method where the candidate with the most votes wins, regardless of whether they receive a majority. Also called plurality voting.

**Gerrymandering.** The manipulation of electoral district boundaries for partisan or personal advantage.

**Instant-runoff voting (IRV).** See ranked choice voting.

**Mixed-member proportional (MMP).** An electoral system combining single-member district elections with proportional representation through party lists, to produce a proportional overall result.

**Monotonicity.** The property of a voting system whereby ranking or scoring a candidate higher can never cause them to lose (and ranking or scoring them lower can never cause them to win).

**Open primary.** A primary election in which any registered voter can participate, regardless of party affiliation.

**Packing.** A gerrymandering technique that concentrates opposing voters into a small number of districts, allowing them to win those districts overwhelmingly while conceding the remaining districts.

**Partisan sorting.** The process by which individuals increasingly align their personal identity, social connections, and policy positions with their party affiliation.

**Plurality voting.** See first-past-the-post.

**Proportional representation.** Any electoral system in which the proportion of seats won by each party closely matches the proportion of votes received.

**Ranked choice voting (RCV).** A voting method where voters rank candidates in order of preference. If no candidate receives a majority of first-choice votes, the last-place candidate is eliminated and their votes are redistributed to voters' next preferences. Also called instant-runoff voting (IRV).

**Score voting.** A voting method where voters rate each candidate on a numerical scale (e.g., 0–5). The candidate with the highest total or average score wins. Also called range voting.

**Single transferable vote (STV).** A proportional representation method used in multi-member districts. Voters rank candidates. Candidates exceeding a quota are elected, and surplus or eliminated-candidate votes transfer to next preferences.

**Spoiler effect.** The phenomenon where a third-party or minor candidate draws votes from a similar major-party candidate, causing both to lose to a less-preferred candidate.

**STAR voting.** Score Then Automatic Runoff — a voting method where voters score candidates 0–5, the top two scorers advance to an automatic runoff, and the finalist preferred by more voters wins.

**Top-two / top-four / top-five primary.** Variants of nonpartisan blanket primaries where the top two, four, or five vote-getters advance to the general election regardless of party.

**Wasted votes.** Votes that do not contribute to electing any representative — either votes for losing candidates or surplus votes beyond what the winning candidate needed.
