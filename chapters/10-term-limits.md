# Chapter 10: Term Limits & Accountability

In 2024, the average age of a U.S. senator was 64 years old. The average tenure was over 11 years. Several members had served for more than 30 years. The Senate included members who had held office since before the internet existed, before smartphones, before most of their youngest constituents were born.

The House wasn't much better. Despite a wave of retirements and primary challenges, dozens of representatives had served 10, 15, even 20+ terms. Some represented districts that had changed beyond recognition during their tenure. Others represented districts drawn specifically to keep them in office forever.

This isn't what representation is supposed to look like.

Term limits are the most popular democracy reform in America — consistently polling above 75% approval across party lines.[^ch10-1] And yet Congress has never imposed them on itself, and the Supreme Court has ruled that states can't impose them on federal officials. It's the rare reform where overwhelming public support meets absolute institutional resistance.

This chapter is about why that support exists, why term limits alone aren't enough, and what a real accountability framework looks like.

## The Case for Term Limits

The argument for term limits is intuitive: **power corrupts, and permanent power corrupts permanently.**

The Founders understood this. The original Articles of Confederation included term limits — delegates could serve no more than three years in any six-year period. Several states had term limits for governors in their original constitutions. The idea that public service should be temporary, not a career, was foundational to the American experiment.

That idea got lost somewhere between 1789 and now.

::: callout
**The incumbency advantage:** In 2024, 94% of incumbent House members who sought reelection won. This isn't because they're all doing a great job. It's because incumbents enjoy massive structural advantages: name recognition, franking privileges (free mail to constituents), gerrymandered districts, established donor networks, and media attention. The system isn't designed to evaluate performance — it's designed to perpetuate tenure.[^ch10-2]
:::

Term limits address several specific problems:

**They break the incumbency cycle.** When an incumbent can't run again, the seat opens up. Open seats attract more candidates, generate more competition, and give voters genuine choices.

**They reduce the power of seniority.** In Congress, committee chairs and leadership positions are awarded primarily based on seniority — how long you've served, not how effectively you've served. This creates a gerontocracy where the most powerful legislators are often the ones who've been in office the longest, regardless of their competence or their alignment with current constituent needs.

**They bring fresh perspectives.** A legislature composed entirely of career politicians develops institutional blindness. New members bring current professional experience, fresh ideas, and connections to the communities they just left. Term limits ensure continuous renewal.

**They weaken special interest capture.** The longer a legislator serves, the deeper their relationships with lobbyists and donors become. Term limits disrupt those relationships before they calcify into dependency.

## The Case Against (And Why It's Partly Right)

Term limits aren't universally praised by reform advocates. The opposition arguments have some merit:

**Loss of institutional knowledge.** Legislating is complex. New members need time to learn the process, understand policy details, and build relationships. If everyone is constantly rotating out, the permanent staff — unelected bureaucrats and lobbyists — accumulate disproportionate power because they're the only ones who know how things work.

::: deep-dive
### The California Cautionary Tale

California imposed strict term limits on its state legislature in 1990 — six years in the Assembly, eight in the Senate. The results were... mixed.

On one hand, the legislature became younger, more diverse, and more reflective of the state's population. Career politicians were replaced by people with recent experience in business, education, and community organizing.

On the other hand, the short terms created problems:

- **Lobbyist influence increased.** With legislators constantly cycling through, Capitol lobbyists became the institutional memory of the legislature. New members relied on them for policy expertise and procedural guidance — exactly the dynamic term limits were supposed to weaken.
- **Musical chairs.** Rather than leaving politics, termed-out legislators simply moved to the other chamber or ran for local office. The "citizen legislator" ideal didn't materialize — career politicians just played a more complex game.
- **Short-term thinking.** Members with limited time in office focused on quick wins over long-term policy development. Why invest in a five-year infrastructure plan when you'll be gone in three?

In 2012, California voters modified the limits — extending the maximum to 12 years total, served in either or both chambers. The revised limits preserved the anti-entrenchment benefit while addressing some of the institutional knowledge concerns.[^ch10-3]
:::

**Voter choice.** The strongest philosophical argument against term limits: if voters want to reelect someone, who are we to tell them they can't? The ballot box is supposed to be the term limit. If an incumbent is doing a good job, forcing them out overrides the will of the voters.

This argument has surface appeal but ignores the structural reality described throughout this book. Voters don't operate in a vacuum. They choose from a pre-filtered set of options, in gerrymandered districts, with massive information asymmetries. The incumbency advantage makes "voter choice" a loaded concept — the choice is between a known quantity with every structural advantage and an unknown challenger who can barely get on the ballot.

**The revolving door accelerates.** If legislators know they'll be looking for a new job in 6-12 years, they may be *more* likely to cultivate relationships with industries that could hire them after their term. Short terms can turn the legislature into a lobbying farm team.

::: callout
**The honest assessment:** Strict term limits with short terms (6-8 years) create real problems. But reasonable term limits (12-18 years) preserve the benefits — preventing 30-year incumbencies, creating regular open-seat elections, and diluting seniority power — while giving members enough time to develop expertise and govern effectively. The goal isn't constant turnover. It's preventing permanent entrenchment.
:::

## Beyond Term Limits: A Real Accountability Framework

Term limits are necessary but not sufficient. They address one dimension of the accountability problem — entrenchment — but leave others untouched. A comprehensive accountability framework would include:

### 1. Term Limits (Done Right)

A reasonable proposal for federal office:

- **House:** Six terms (12 years)
- **Senate:** Three terms (18 years)
- **Lifetime cap:** 24 years of combined service in Congress

This gives members enough time to develop genuine expertise while preventing the 30- and 40-year careers that characterize the current system. A 12-year House tenure allows a member to chair a committee, develop policy expertise, and build coalitions — but not to become a permanent fixture whose power derives from longevity rather than performance.

::: deep-dive
### The Constitutional Problem

In *U.S. Term Limits, Inc. v. Thornton* (1995), the Supreme Court ruled 5-4 that states cannot impose additional qualifications for federal office beyond those specified in the Constitution (age, citizenship, and residency). Since the Constitution doesn't include term limits for Congress, only a constitutional amendment can impose them.

This is a significant barrier. Constitutional amendments require two-thirds approval in both chambers of Congress and ratification by three-fourths of state legislatures. Asking Congress to limit its own tenure is asking turkeys to vote for Thanksgiving — again.

But there's a workaround: **Article V convention.** The Constitution allows two-thirds of state legislatures (34 states) to call a constitutional convention to propose amendments. As of 2024, several states have passed resolutions calling for a convention to consider term limits, though the total falls well short of 34.[^ch10-4]

The convention path is controversial — some scholars worry about a "runaway convention" that could propose radical constitutional changes beyond its original scope. But proponents argue that the convention can be limited to a single subject (term limits) and that the ratification requirement (38 states) provides a safety net against extreme proposals.
:::

### 2. Performance Transparency

Term limits address how *long* someone serves. They don't address how *well* they serve. A real accountability framework needs tools for voters to evaluate performance:

**Legislative scorecards.** Independent organizations already produce voting scorecards, but they're usually ideological — tracking how often a member votes conservative or progressive. What we need are *effectiveness* scorecards:

- How many bills did the member introduce that became law?
- How often do they attend committee hearings?
- How frequently do they hold town halls or constituent meetings?
- What's their bipartisan cooperation score?
- How do they spend their office budget?

::: callout
**The Lugar Center-Georgetown McCourt School Bipartisan Index** ranks every member of Congress by how often they work across party lines — co-sponsoring bills with members of the other party and attracting bipartisan support for their own legislation. In a healthy democracy, this would be headline news. Instead, it's an obscure academic exercise that most voters have never heard of.[^ch10-5]
:::

**Financial transparency.** Members of Congress are required to file financial disclosures, but the process is slow, the data is hard to access, and enforcement is weak. The STOCK Act (2012) was supposed to prevent insider trading by members of Congress, but compliance has been spotty and penalties have been trivial. Real accountability requires real-time, machine-readable financial disclosures — not paper forms filed months after the fact.

### 3. Recall Mechanisms

Eighteen states allow voters to recall elected state officials through a petition and special election process. No mechanism exists for recalling federal officials — the Constitution provides only for expulsion by a two-thirds vote of the relevant chamber, which almost never happens.

A recall mechanism for federal officials would be politically complex and constitutionally challenging. But the principle is sound: if an elected official fundamentally betrays the trust of their constituents, the constituents shouldn't have to wait 2-6 years for the next scheduled election to do something about it.

### 4. Ethics Enforcement with Teeth

The House and Senate Ethics Committees are the internal enforcement bodies for congressional conduct. They are, to put it charitably, ineffective.

Both committees are evenly split between parties — ensuring that neither party will aggressively investigate the other for fear of retaliation. Investigations are slow, opaque, and rarely result in meaningful consequences. The most common outcome is a private letter of admonishment that the public never sees.

::: deep-dive
### A Better Model: Independent Ethics Enforcement

Several democracies have independent ethics bodies that operate outside the legislature:

**The UK's Independent Parliamentary Standards Authority (IPSA)** sets and administers MP compensation and expenses, removing a major source of self-dealing. It was created in 2009 after the MPs' expenses scandal revealed widespread abuse.

**Canada's Conflict of Interest and Ethics Commissioner** is an independent officer of Parliament who investigates complaints, issues reports, and makes recommendations that are public by default.

**The U.S. Office of Congressional Ethics (OCE)** was created in 2008 as an independent body that could review complaints and refer them to the House Ethics Committee. But the OCE can only recommend — it can't enforce. And the House Ethics Committee has repeatedly tried to defund or limit it.

A truly independent congressional ethics body would have subpoena power, the authority to impose meaningful penalties, and complete independence from the legislators it oversees. This is not a radical proposal — it's how ethics enforcement works in most professional contexts. Lawyers are disciplined by bar associations, doctors by medical boards, accountants by regulatory bodies. Only politicians are left to police themselves.[^ch10-6]
:::

### 5. Voter Education Infrastructure

Accountability requires informed voters. And informed voting requires information that's accessible, reliable, and nonpartisan.

Several nonprofits already do this work — Vote Smart, Ballotpedia, and the League of Women Voters produce candidate information guides. But their reach is limited, their funding is precarious, and they compete with a flood of partisan misinformation.

A public investment in voter education — perhaps funded through the same mechanism as democracy dollars — could ensure that every voter has access to:

- Unbiased candidate profiles and voting records
- Clear explanations of ballot measures
- Contact information for elected officials
- Tools for comparing candidates on specific issues

This isn't about telling voters *what* to think. It's about ensuring they have the raw materials to think at all.

## The Accountability Ecosystem

Term limits, transparency, ethics enforcement, and voter education are not independent reforms. They form an ecosystem:

- **Term limits** ensure regular turnover, creating competitive elections
- **Performance transparency** gives voters the information to evaluate candidates in those elections
- **Ethics enforcement** catches misconduct between elections
- **Voter education** ensures the public can use all of these tools effectively

Remove any one piece and the others are weakened. A termed-out legislator replaced by an equally unaccountable successor hasn't improved anything. An ethics body investigating a legislator that voters can't vote out hasn't solved the problem. The pieces work together or they don't work at all.

::: callout
**The bottom line:** Term limits are the most popular reform in America for a reason — people intuitively understand that permanent power is dangerous. But term limits alone aren't enough. What we need is a comprehensive accountability framework that limits tenure, demands transparency, enforces ethics, and empowers voters. The goal isn't to punish politicians. It's to create a system where good governance is rewarded and bad governance has consequences. That's not radical. That's the job description.
:::
