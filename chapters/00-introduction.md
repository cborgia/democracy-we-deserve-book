# Introduction {.unnumbered}

The number one selling vehicle in America is a pickup truck.

Not a sedan. Not an SUV. Not a practical little hatchback that gets 40 miles to the gallon. A full-size, bed-in-the-back, tow-anything pickup truck.

And here's the thing — most people who buy one don't haul anything on a daily basis. They commute to work. They pick up groceries. They drive their kids to school. By any rational, utilitarian measure, a pickup truck is overkill for 90% of what most owners actually do with it.

So why do they buy it?

**Possibilities.**

A pickup truck means that *if* you want to bring home a new grill from Lowe's, you can. *If* your buddy needs help moving a couch, you're the one who gets the call. *If* you decide on a Saturday morning to build a deck, you can load up the lumber yourself. You might only use that bed a dozen times a year — but every single day, you know it's there. The capability exists. The option is open. The possibility is real.

Americans don't buy trucks for what they do every day. They buy trucks for what they *could* do on any given day. And that distinction — between the routine and the possible — is one of the most powerful forces in American culture.

We are a nation built on possibility.

Think about it. The kid in the garage who drops out of college to start a company — that's not rational. It's possible. The family that packs everything into a U-Haul and moves across the country for a fresh start — that's not safe. It's possible. The immigrant who arrives with nothing and opens a business — that's not easy. It's possible.

We don't just tolerate possibility in this country. We worship it. It's the engine of the American dream. It's why we start more businesses per capita than almost any other nation. It's why we still attract the world's most ambitious and creative people. It's why "land of opportunity" isn't just a slogan — it's the operating principle of 330 million people who believe, against all evidence and all odds, that tomorrow can be better than today.

::: callout
**The possibility instinct isn't naïve — it's productive.** Americans aren't dreamers who ignore reality. They're pragmatists who expand it. The Apollo program wasn't a fantasy — it was a nation deciding that landing on the moon was possible and then engineering the solution. The interstate highway system wasn't a wish — it was the belief that connecting every city in America by road was possible, followed by the largest public works project in history. Possibility, when taken seriously, becomes a blueprint.
:::

So here's my question: if we bring that instinct — that relentless, stubborn belief in what's possible — to everything else in our lives, why do we leave it at the door when it comes to our own government?

## The Exception

We expect possibility everywhere — except in democracy.

When your phone gets slow, you upgrade it. When your car breaks down, you fix it or get a better one. When your business hits a wall, you pivot, adapt, innovate. Americans do not accept "that's just how it works" in any other area of life.

But in politics? "That's just how it works" is the national anthem.

Two parties? That's just how it works. Gerrymandered districts? That's just how it works. Gridlock? Corruption? Representatives who answer to donors instead of voters? Shrug. That's just how it works.

We have accepted a level of dysfunction in our government that we would never tolerate in our businesses, our technology, our sports teams, or even our restaurants. If your favorite restaurant served bad food and blamed it on the kitchen layout, you'd say "then fix the kitchen." You wouldn't keep eating there for 50 years, complaining the whole time, and accepting the excuse that the building was designed wrong and nothing can be done.

But that's exactly what we do with democracy.

::: deep-dive
### The Renovation Analogy

Think of it like a house. You buy a house built in 1789. It's a good house — solid foundation, revolutionary design for its time. But it's 2026 now. The wiring is outdated. The plumbing leaks. The layout doesn't work for how you actually live. Some rooms are great. Others haven't been updated since the Eisenhower administration.

You don't tear the house down. You renovate.

You keep the foundation — it's strong. You keep the best features — the craftsmanship, the bones, the character. But you update the systems. You rewire. You replumb. You knock out a wall that doesn't make sense anymore. You add insulation, upgrade the windows, maybe put solar on the roof.

That's what this book is about. Not tearing down American democracy. Renovating it. Keeping what works. Fixing what doesn't. Upgrading the systems to match the demands of the century we actually live in.

The Founders built an extraordinary house. But they built it for 4 million people in 13 states with horses and quill pens. We're 330 million people in 50 states with supercomputers in our pockets. It's time for a renovation.
:::

## What's Possible

This book isn't a complaint. It's not a diagnosis of decline or a doom-and-gloom warning about the death of democracy. There are enough of those books. You don't need another one.

This book is about **what's possible.**

It's possible to have elections where you don't hold your nose and pick the lesser evil — where you actually express your full preferences and the candidate with the broadest support wins. Chapter 5 shows you how.

It's possible to have primaries where every voter participates — not just the most extreme 15% of one party — and candidates have to earn support from all of their constituents. Chapter 6 lays it out.

It's possible to have congressional districts drawn by citizens instead of politicians — maps designed for fair representation, not guaranteed outcomes. Chapters 4 and 9 prove it, with real examples from real states.

It's possible to have a Congress with five or six parties, where governing requires coalition and compromise instead of performance and obstruction. Chapter 7 shows that most of the world already works this way — and it works better.

It's possible to have campaigns funded by ordinary voters — every citizen holding $100 in democracy dollars, every neighborhood worth a candidate's time. Chapter 8 shows it happening right now, in Seattle.

It's possible to have representatives who serve the public for a limited time and then go home — instead of building 30-year empires funded by lobbyists. Chapter 10 makes the case.

None of these ideas are theoretical. None of them are untested. Every single one is operating somewhere in America *right now* — in cities, in states, in communities where citizens decided that "that's just how it works" wasn't good enough.

::: callout
**Possibility isn't passive.** The family that buys the pickup truck doesn't just *wish* they could haul a couch — they *can*. The capability is real. The reforms in this book aren't wishes. They're capabilities. Proven tools, sitting on the shelf, waiting to be used. The only question is whether we pick them up.
:::

## The Pickup Truck Test

Here's a framework I want you to carry through this book. When you read about a reform — score voting, open primaries, independent redistricting, any of them — ask yourself the pickup truck question:

**Does this expand what's possible?**

Not "is this perfect?" Not "will this solve everything?" Not "has this ever failed anywhere?" Those are important questions, and we'll address them honestly. But the first question — the American question — is about possibility.

Does score voting make it *possible* for more than two parties to compete? Yes.

Do open primaries make it *possible* for the full electorate to choose candidates? Yes.

Does independent redistricting make it *possible* for elections to be genuinely competitive? Yes.

Do democracy dollars make it *possible* for ordinary people to have a voice in campaign finance? Yes.

Every reform in this book passes the pickup truck test. It expands what's possible. It gives citizens capabilities they don't currently have. And just like that truck in your driveway — you might not use every capability every day. But knowing it's there changes everything.

## Who This Book Is For

This book is for the 67% of Americans who tell pollsters they're dissatisfied with the way democracy works — but don't know what to do about it.[^intro-1]

It's for the independent voter who's tired of being told they're "throwing away" their vote.

It's for the Republican who doesn't recognize their party anymore and the Democrat who feels the same way.

It's for the young voter who looks at Congress and sees an institution that looks, acts, and thinks nothing like the country they live in.

It's for the civics teacher who wants to tell students that the system can work — and needs evidence to back it up.

It's for anyone who has ever said "there has to be a better way" and wondered if there actually is.

There is. You're holding it.

::: deep-dive
### A Note on Partisanship

This is not a partisan book. The structural failures described in these pages hurt voters across the political spectrum. Gerrymandering is used by both parties. Closed primaries exclude independents regardless of which party controls them. The campaign finance system serves donors over voters whether those donors lean left or right.

The reforms in this book have been championed by conservatives, progressives, and independents. Alaska's open primary was supported by a coalition that included Republican business leaders and Democratic tribal organizations. Michigan's redistricting commission was approved by voters in every congressional district — urban and rural, liberal and conservative. Campaign finance reform polls above 70% across party lines.

If you came here looking for someone to blame, you'll find plenty of blame to go around. But the villains in this story are incentive structures, not people. Fix the incentives and the people — the same people — produce better results.

This is a book about systems. And systems can be redesigned.
:::

## How to Read This Book

**Part I: The Problem** takes apart the current system — not to depress you, but to show you exactly what's broken and why. You can't fix what you don't understand.

**Part II: The Solutions** is the heart of the book. Each chapter presents a proven reform: what it is, how it works, where it's already working, and what the evidence says. This is the toolkit.

**Part III: Making It Happen** is the playbook. Which states are leading? What can you do, starting today? And what does the democracy we deserve actually look like?

You don't have to read it in order. If you already understand the problem and want solutions, skip to Part II. If you want to get involved right now, jump to Chapter 12. If you want the big picture and the emotional case, read Chapter 13 first.

But wherever you start, carry this with you: **the belief that better is possible.** Not inevitable. Not easy. Possible.

Americans have always been at their best when they decide something is possible and then refuse to accept anything less. We put a man on the moon because we decided it was possible. We built the internet because we decided it was possible. We expanded the right to vote — again and again, over two centuries — because we decided it was possible.

A better democracy is possible. This book shows you how.

Let's get started.
