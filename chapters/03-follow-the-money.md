# Chapter 3: Follow the Money

In 2024, the total cost of federal elections in the United States exceeded $15.4 billion.[^ch3-1] That's billion, with a B. Presidential and congressional races combined — more money than the GDP of Iceland.

Where did it come from? Who gave it? And what did they get in return?

These aren't cynical questions. They're the most important questions in American democracy — because the answers reveal who actually has power in our system, and it's not the voters.

## The Numbers

Let's start with what the money looks like.

The average winning U.S. Senate campaign in 2024 cost approximately $26 million. The average winning House campaign cost roughly $2.8 million.[^ch3-2] These aren't outliers — they're *averages*. Competitive races cost far more. The most expensive Senate race in 2024 topped $400 million in combined spending between the candidates, parties, and outside groups.

To raise $26 million for a Senate campaign, a candidate would need to raise roughly $14,000 *every single day* for the entire six-year term. That's not a job description for a legislator. It's a job description for a fundraiser.

::: callout
**The fundraising treadmill:** Former members of Congress have reported spending 30–70% of their time in office fundraising — calling donors, attending fundraising events, and courting potential contributors. In 2013, a leaked PowerPoint from the Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee recommended that new members spend *four hours per day* on "call time" — cold-calling potential donors. Four hours. Every day. While supposedly governing.[^ch3-3]
:::

This isn't a side effect of the system. It *is* the system. The first filter for who can represent you in government isn't "Are they qualified?" or "Do they share your values?" It's "Can they raise enough money?"

## Who's Giving

Here's where it gets worse.

In theory, campaigns are funded by ordinary citizens exercising their democratic voice through small donations. In practice, the money comes overwhelmingly from a tiny sliver of the population.

In the 2024 election cycle:

- **0.47%** of the U.S. population made contributions of $200 or more to federal candidates or PACs
- The top **100 donors** gave more than the bottom **4.75 million small donors combined**
- Super PACs — which can accept unlimited contributions — received the majority of their funding from fewer than **2,000 individuals**[^ch3-4]

::: deep-dive
### The Donor Class

Political scientists have a term for the tiny fraction of Americans who fund elections: the "donor class." And the donor class looks nothing like the rest of America.

Donors giving $1,000 or more are overwhelmingly white, male, older, and wealthy. Their policy priorities diverge sharply from the general public. They're more likely to favor deregulation, lower capital gains taxes, and free trade — and less likely to prioritize minimum wage increases, healthcare expansion, or climate action.

This matters because research consistently shows that **elected officials are far more responsive to the preferences of their donors than to the preferences of their constituents.**

A landmark 2014 study by Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page examined 1,779 policy issues over 20 years and found that the preferences of average citizens had "a minuscule, near-zero, statistically non-significant impact upon public policy." The preferences of economic elites and organized interest groups, by contrast, had substantial and significant influence.[^ch3-5]

In other words: the people who fund campaigns get the government they paid for. Everyone else gets whatever's left.
:::

## Citizens United and the Flood

To understand how we got here, you need to understand *Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission* — the 2010 Supreme Court decision that fundamentally reshaped American campaign finance.

The case itself involved a conservative nonprofit that wanted to air a documentary critical of Hillary Clinton during the 2008 primary season. The FEC said this counted as an "electioneering communication" and was restricted under the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act (McCain-Feingold).

The Supreme Court ruled 5–4 that restricting independent political expenditures by corporations and unions violated the First Amendment. The reasoning: money is speech, corporations have First Amendment rights, and the government cannot restrict independent spending on political speech.

::: callout
**The key legal fiction:** The Court held that independent expenditures — spending that isn't coordinated with a campaign — "do not give rise to corruption or the appearance of corruption." Therefore, they cannot be limited.

This is, to put it politely, divorced from reality. When a billionaire spends $50 million on ads supporting a candidate, and that candidate wins, the idea that no corrupting influence exists because the spending was technically "independent" requires a level of naiveté that even the billionaire would find amusing.[^ch3-6]
:::

Citizens United didn't create the problem of money in politics. Money has shaped American elections since the founding. But it removed the guardrails. In the years following the decision:

- **Super PACs** proliferated — organizations that can raise unlimited sums from individuals, corporations, and unions, as long as they don't "coordinate" with campaigns (a restriction so loosely enforced it's essentially meaningless)
- **Dark money** exploded — spending by nonprofits that aren't required to disclose their donors, routed through 501(c)(4) organizations
- **Total outside spending** went from $338 million in 2008 to over $2.6 billion in 2024[^ch3-7]

The decision didn't open a door. It removed a wall.

## The Access Economy

Let's be precise about what money buys in politics, because it's not as crude as "I give you $10,000 and you vote my way." That happens, but it's the exception. The real mechanism is more subtle and more pervasive.

Money buys **access.**

A $5,000 donor gets a handshake at a fundraiser. A $50,000 donor gets a private meeting. A $500,000 Super PAC contributor gets the candidate's personal cell phone number. And when that donor calls about a bill working through committee, the call gets returned. When your constituent calls about the same bill, they get a form email from a 23-year-old intern.

::: deep-dive
### The Revolving Door

The money in politics story doesn't end with campaign contributions. It extends to what happens *after* elected officials leave office.

Between 2000 and 2024, more than half of all former members of Congress went on to work as lobbyists or in lobbying-adjacent roles.[^ch3-8] The career path is explicit: serve in Congress, build relationships and expertise, then cash out by selling that access to the highest bidder.

This creates a perverse incentive while *in* office. A member of Congress considering a vote on banking regulation knows that their post-congressional career may depend on staying in the good graces of the financial industry. They don't need to be bribed. The future payoff is implicit — and perfectly legal.

Lobbying firms in Washington, D.C. generated over $4.3 billion in revenue in 2024.[^ch3-9] That's not charity. That's a return on investment. And the investors wouldn't keep paying if it didn't work.
:::

## The Legislative Marketplace

The effect on actual legislation is measurable.

Consider a few examples:

**Pharmaceutical pricing.** Polls consistently show that 80%+ of Americans support allowing Medicare to negotiate drug prices. For decades, Congress refused to act. Why? The pharmaceutical industry spent $374 million on lobbying in 2022 alone — more than any other industry — and contributed tens of millions to congressional campaigns on both sides.[^ch3-10] When a limited version of drug price negotiation finally passed in 2022 (the Inflation Reduction Act), it was restricted to just 10 drugs initially, with implementation delayed years into the future.

**Gun policy.** Universal background checks for gun purchases have had 85-90% public support for over a decade. Congress has repeatedly failed to pass them. The gun lobby — led by the NRA and Gun Owners of America — spends relatively modest amounts on direct contributions but wields enormous influence through independent expenditures and an organized, highly motivated donor base that punishes any deviation from its positions.

**Tax policy.** The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act — which primarily benefited corporations and high-income earners — was drafted, passed, and signed into law in less than eight weeks. Compare that to the years-long struggle to pass even modest expansions of the child tax credit, which benefits lower- and middle-income families.

::: callout
**The donor test:** When a policy has overwhelming public support but can't pass Congress, or an unpopular policy sails through with bipartisan speed, apply the donor test: Who benefits financially? Follow the money, and the legislative mystery usually solves itself.
:::

## Small Dollar Myths

"But what about small-dollar fundraising?" you might ask. "Didn't candidates like Bernie Sanders and Barack Obama prove that you can run on grassroots donations?"

Yes and no.

Small-dollar fundraising has grown dramatically. ActBlue (Democratic) and WinRed (Republican) have made it trivially easy to donate $5 or $25 to a candidate you saw on social media. In 2024, small-dollar donations (under $200) made up a significant portion of total fundraising.

But small-dollar fundraising hasn't replaced big money — it's been layered on top of it. Super PACs still spend billions. Dark money still flows. And small-dollar fundraising has its own distortions: it rewards the most provocative, attention-grabbing candidates — the ones who generate viral moments and outrage — because that's what drives small online donations.

The most extreme members of Congress are often the *best* small-dollar fundraisers. Saying something inflammatory on cable news can generate $1 million in donations overnight. This doesn't reduce polarization — it monetizes it.

::: deep-dive
### The Attention Tax

Small-dollar fundraising has created what you might call an "attention tax" on governance. Members of Congress now have a financial incentive to generate controversy, because controversy generates donations.

A thoughtful legislator who quietly builds coalitions and passes bipartisan bills generates no viral moments and raises little money. A bomb-thrower who tweets provocative statements, picks fights on the House floor, and goes on cable news generates millions.

The incentive structure rewards performance over governance — even when the money comes from ordinary people in $25 increments. The system corrupts not just through the ultra-wealthy, but through the attention economy itself.[^ch3-11]
:::

## What Reform Looks Like

Campaign finance reform is where most people's minds go first when they think about fixing democracy. And the instinct is right — money is corrupting the system. But the path to reform is complicated by two decades of Supreme Court decisions that have systematically dismantled limits on political spending.

The most promising approaches don't try to *limit* spending (which the courts have blocked). Instead, they try to **amplify the power of ordinary voters' money** to counter the influence of big donors.

Chapter 8 will explore these in detail — particularly **democracy dollars**, a system that gives every voter public funds to donate to candidates of their choice — but here's the framework:

1. **Public financing systems** that match small donations with public funds, making a $25 donation worth $150 or more
2. **Democracy vouchers** or democracy dollars that give every registered voter a set amount (say, $100) to allocate to candidates
3. **Disclosure requirements** that force dark money into the light — you can spend what you want, but voters get to see who's paying
4. **Anti-bundling rules** that limit the ability of lobbyists and fundraisers to aggregate contributions for outsized influence

These approaches work *within* the current legal framework. They don't require overturning Citizens United (though that would help). They change the incentive structure by making ordinary voters' money count more — not by making wealthy donors' money count less.

::: callout
**Seattle's experiment:** In 2017, Seattle became the first city in the U.S. to implement a democracy voucher program. Every registered voter received four $25 vouchers to donate to qualifying city council candidates. In its first cycle, the program tripled the number of donors participating in city elections and dramatically diversified the donor pool — more women, more people of color, more low-income residents contributed to campaigns than ever before.[^ch3-12]
:::

The money problem in American democracy is real, it's enormous, and it won't fix itself. But it's also solvable — not by getting money out of politics (the courts won't allow it), but by putting *more people's money* into politics and making transparency the price of participation.
