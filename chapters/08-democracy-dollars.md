# Chapter 8: Democracy Dollars & Campaign Finance Reform

Chapter 3 laid out the problem: elections cost billions, the money comes from a tiny fraction of the population, and the result is a government that's more responsive to donors than to voters. The Supreme Court has made it clear that limiting spending is off the table. So what do we do?

We stop trying to drain the ocean and start raising the tide.

The most promising campaign finance reforms don't try to take money *out* of politics — they put *more people's money* in. The goal is simple: if you can't reduce the influence of big donors (thanks, Citizens United), you can dilute it by dramatically expanding the donor pool.

And the most powerful tool in that toolkit is **democracy dollars.**

## The Concept

Democracy dollars — sometimes called democracy vouchers — give every registered voter a fixed amount of public money to donate to the candidates of their choice. The details vary by implementation, but the core idea is consistent:

- Every voter receives vouchers (typically $25–$100 each, totaling $50–$200 per voter per cycle)
- Vouchers can be donated to any qualifying candidate in any race
- Candidates who accept vouchers agree to certain conditions (usually limits on accepting large private donations)
- Unused vouchers expire at the end of the election cycle

::: callout
**The math of people power:** There are approximately 240 million eligible voters in the United States. If each received $100 in democracy dollars per two-year election cycle, that's $24 billion in potential small-donor funding — dwarfing the approximately $6 billion in private contributions to federal candidates in 2024. Even if only 30% of voters used their vouchers, that's still $7.2 billion in citizen-directed funding.

The donor class currently wins because they're the only ones at the table. Democracy dollars put everyone at the table.[^ch8-1]
:::

## Seattle: Proof of Concept

In 2015, Seattle voters approved the nation's first democracy voucher program. Starting in 2017, every registered voter in Seattle received four $25 vouchers ($100 total) to donate to qualifying candidates for city council and city attorney.

The results were immediate and striking:

**The donor pool exploded.** In the 2017 city council races, the number of people contributing to campaigns nearly *tripled* compared to previous cycles. Thousands of first-time donors participated — people who had never given to a campaign before.[^ch8-2]

**The donor pool diversified.** Democracy voucher users were significantly more representative of Seattle's population than traditional donors. More women, more people of color, more low-income residents, and more young people participated. The donor class started looking like the city itself.

**Campaign behavior changed.** Candidates who relied on voucher funding spent more time in low-income and minority neighborhoods — areas traditionally ignored by campaigns because they didn't produce big-dollar fundraisers. When every voter holds $100, every neighborhood is worth canvassing.

::: deep-dive
### The Data on Seattle

Researchers have studied Seattle's program extensively. Key findings:

**Participation rates:** In its first cycle (2017), approximately 3.3% of eligible Seattle residents used at least one voucher. By 2023, that number had grown to over 10%. These rates far exceed traditional small-donor participation rates in comparable cities.

**Demographic shift:** Voucher donors were 30% more likely to live in low-income zip codes than traditional donors. They were more racially diverse and younger. The median voucher donor lived in a neighborhood with a median household income $20,000 lower than the median traditional donor.

**Candidate behavior:** Candidates in the voucher program held 40% more public campaign events in low-income areas compared to non-participating candidates. They also spent more time on retail campaigning (knocking doors, attending community events) and less time on high-dollar fundraising calls.

**Electoral outcomes:** Several candidates who relied primarily on voucher funding won their races, including candidates who would have been uncompetitive under the traditional fundraising model — particularly candidates of color and first-time candidates without existing donor networks.[^ch8-3]

The program isn't perfect. Administrative costs are real. Some vouchers go unused. Gaming concerns exist. But the fundamental hypothesis — that giving ordinary people money to spend on politics changes who participates and who wins — has been validated.
:::

## Other Models

Democracy dollars aren't the only approach to amplifying small-donor power. Several related systems are in use or have been proposed:

### Public Matching Funds

Small donations are matched with public money at a fixed ratio. New York City's system — one of the oldest and most studied — matches the first $175 of each donation at an 8:1 ratio. That means a $10 donation becomes $90. A $175 donation becomes $1,575.

::: callout
**New York City's matching program** has been in place since 1989 and is considered the gold standard for public matching systems. Participating candidates (which includes most serious contenders) must agree to spending limits and additional disclosure requirements. The program has been credited with increasing candidate diversity, reducing reliance on large donors, and enabling competitive races in communities that were previously ignored by campaigns.[^ch8-4]
:::

**Strengths:** Leverages existing donation behavior. Doesn't require creating a voucher system from scratch. Rewards candidates who build broad small-donor coalitions.

**Weaknesses:** Still requires voters to donate their own money first — which means lower-income voters participate less. The matching ratio has to be high enough (6:1 or 8:1) to meaningfully compete with large donations. Can be expensive for the public treasury.

### Tax Credits for Political Donations

Several states offer tax credits (not deductions) for small political donations. Minnesota's Political Contribution Refund program refunded up to $50 per person ($100 per couple) for donations to state candidates — effectively making the first $50 of political giving free.

**Strengths:** Simple to administer. Uses existing tax infrastructure.

**Weaknesses:** Only benefits people who file taxes and know about the program. Skews toward higher-income voters who are more likely to donate in the first place. Minnesota's program was suspended in 2016 due to budget pressures.

### Clean Elections / Full Public Financing

A few states and cities have experimented with fully publicly funded elections. Qualifying candidates who demonstrate a threshold of community support (usually through collecting a minimum number of small donations) receive a lump sum of public money and agree not to raise private funds.

Maine and Arizona both adopted clean elections programs in the late 1990s. They produced some notable successes — candidates from underrepresented backgrounds winning offices they never could have afforded to contest — but both programs have been weakened over time by legislative changes and court decisions limiting their scope.[^ch8-5]

## Why Democracy Dollars Are the Best Option

Of all these approaches, democracy dollars have the strongest combination of features:

**Universal participation.** Every voter gets vouchers — not just those who can afford to donate. This is the critical difference from matching systems, which still require an initial financial commitment that lower-income voters may not make.

**Voter agency.** Unlike full public financing (where a lump sum goes to qualifying candidates), democracy dollars let individual voters decide where the money goes. This preserves the signal function of donations — candidates have to earn support — while democratizing who gets to send the signal.

**Candidate incentives.** When every voter is a potential $100 donor, candidates have a financial reason to engage with *all* communities, not just wealthy ones. This changes who runs, how they campaign, and who they listen to once in office.

**Political viability.** Democracy dollars are easier to sell politically than full public financing ("why should my taxes pay for politicians?") because voters control the money. It's not a government handout to politicians — it's a citizen empowerment tool.

::: deep-dive
### Objections and Responses

**"It's too expensive."** The cost depends on participation rates and voucher amounts. Seattle's program costs approximately $6 million per election cycle for a city of 750,000. Scaled nationally with $100 per voter, a federal program would cost $24 billion at full participation — but likely $7-8 billion at realistic participation rates. That's real money, but it's less than 0.2% of the federal budget, and it's an investment in the integrity of the democratic process.

**"People will sell their vouchers."** Seattle's program included anti-fraud measures (vouchers are non-transferable and tied to voter registration). There have been isolated cases of voucher fraud, but nothing systemic. The program includes penalties for misuse.

**"It won't change anything — big money will still dominate."** This undersells the structural impact. When candidates can raise competitive funding from vouchers alone, they have a genuine *choice* about whether to court big donors. Some will still take big money. But some won't — and voters will be able to see the difference and reward candidates who rely on people-powered funding.

**"Why should non-citizens / non-voters get vouchers?"** Most proposals limit vouchers to registered voters. This is a design choice — some advocates argue for broader eligibility (all residents, including non-citizen taxpayers) while others want to tie it strictly to voter registration as an additional incentive to register.[^ch8-6]
:::

## The Path from Seattle to the Nation

Seattle's program applies only to city elections. Scaling democracy dollars to the federal level faces political and logistical challenges, but the path is clear:

**Step 1: More cities.** As more municipalities adopt democracy voucher programs and publish results, the evidence base grows and the concept becomes familiar. Oakland, California passed a democracy voucher measure in 2022.

**Step 2: States.** State-level democracy dollar programs would cover governor, state legislature, and other statewide races. This is where the concept gets seriously tested at scale.

**Step 3: Federal legislation.** Multiple bills have been introduced in Congress that include democracy dollar provisions, including the For the People Act (H.R. 1). Federal implementation would likely give every voter $100-$200 in vouchers for federal races each cycle.

::: callout
**The For the People Act** (H.R. 1 / S. 1), introduced in multiple sessions of Congress, includes a 6:1 public matching system for small donations to federal candidates. While not a full democracy dollar program, it represents the closest Congress has come to legislating small-donor empowerment at the federal level. The bill passed the House in 2019 and 2021 but was blocked in the Senate.[^ch8-7]
:::

## Campaign Finance and the Other Reforms

Democracy dollars don't work in isolation. They're most powerful when combined with the other reforms in this book:

**Open primaries + democracy dollars** = Candidates in open primaries need broad appeal. Democracy dollars ensure they have a financial path to reach the full electorate, not just party donors.

**Score voting + democracy dollars** = When voters can support multiple candidates (by scoring them), candidates funded by democracy dollars can run without the spoiler dynamic. More candidates, more choice, more diversity — funded by the people.

**Proportional representation + democracy dollars** = In a multi-party system, democracy dollars prevent any single party's donor base from dominating. Smaller parties can compete financially if they have genuine voter support.

Each reform reinforces the others. That's the design. Democracy isn't one fix — it's a system of interlocking structures, and when you repair one piece, it makes the others work better.

The money problem in American politics is enormous. But the solution isn't to fight the money. It's to put money in the hands of the people and let them fight for themselves.
